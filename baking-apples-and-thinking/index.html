Baking apples and thinking about turkey | Sweet Basil and the Bee

  • Post author By Allen Harris
  • Post date November 13, 2019

Baked an apple lately? I revisited this childhood treat a week ago and was surprised by how effortless and tasty it was! It’s an old-fashioned, simple dessert with all the flavor of an apple pie or apple crisp, but no peeling, slicing, or pie crust-making. It can be a rustic dessert, an elegant dessert swaddled in heavy cream and bourbon, or reheated for breakfast. An article by Ann Stockwell on Epicurious last November taught me some refinements on my mom’s “golden-delicious-apples-in-a-brownie-pan” version which had great flavor but was kind of slumped and mushy.

The variety of apple you use makes a big difference, and Stockwell took the time to bake eight different varieties — Jonagold, honeycrisp, Cortland, Granny Smith, gala, pink lady, Fuji and McIntosh — and report on the findings. The winners, gala and Fuji, were ideal: soft enough inside to eat with a spoon, but they retained their shape when baked.

You need one apple per person, and it doesn’t matter how many you bake at once. Choose a baking dish that will hold the number of apples you choose without being crowded or having too much space between. I cooked ours in individual ramekins which made them feel like the dessert course delivered by a waiter in a chi-chi restaurant.

Use a paring knife to slice around the top core of the apple, then pull out what you’ve cut. Don’t use an apple corer or cut all the way through to the bottom – just make a nice little volcano crater in the top to hold some goodies. Stockwell uses the sharp edge of a measuring spoon or melon baller to make her crater.

Place the apples in a baking dish with sides about 2-inches high, then fill the crater with sweet things. I used raisins, a teaspoon of brown sugar, a pinch of salt and a knob of Kerrygold butter. Other possibilities include coconut sugar, maple sugar, maple syrup or honey. You can also add apple pie spices like cinnamon, nutmeg, allspice, clove, cardamom and ginger, but it’s not necessary. It doesn’t matter what order the filling stuff goes in; it all just melts together as it bakes.

Pour a little liquid around the apples to keep them from sticking — about an inch of water in a baking pan, less in a ramekin. The water gets seasoned by all the goodies in the apples, but if you want to upgrade use apple cider and maybe a splash of bourbon or brandy for the liquid.

Bake the apples in a 350 degree oven until they are very tender — about 45 minutes to an hour. Check on them in half an hour — if the liquid has evaporated and the pan is dry, add more. Let them sit, out of the oven for 5-10 minutes before serving because they are mouth-scorching hot. Spoon some of the pan liquid over each apple in its bowl and add a splash of cold heavy cream, or ice cream. The tartness of the warm Fuji apple combined with the butter and brown sugar and cold cream is heavenly — better than pie, which can be bland, or apple crisp which is often too sweet.

Two weeks until Thanksgiving and after years of having the restaurant Pluto’s roast my turkey, I’m cast back on the tall stack of clippings of turkey-roasting fads and ideas. I can easily eliminate deep frying, smoking or grilling, but as this is my first Thanksgiving as a Grandmother I do want a juicy bird with crispy skin and a kitchen that smells like a proper Thanksgiving.

Brining is an attractive idea but the logistics of submerging a 15-pound turkey in saltwater and keeping it refrigerated are daunting with all the other ingredients vying for refrigerator space. I ran into this dry-brined recipe on the Genius Recipes website (food52.com) that has great credentials and it will be the one I’m working with this Thanksgiving. Called the Russ Parson’s Dry-Brined Turkey (aka the Judy Bird), it won a taste test with the L.A. Times Food Section staff in 2006 and Russ Parsons, food editor at the time has been writing about it ever since.

“The technique is inspired by chef Judy Rodgers, who dry-brines the famous roast chicken (and just about everything else) at Zuni Café in San Francisco, but never a turkey. Parsons decided to try it and found that, not only does it work — it also comes out perfectly juicy and crisp, with none of the sponginess that you sometimes get with wet-brined birds.” Parsons also discovered you can dry brine a frozen turkey while it’s defrosting. Here’s the recipe, (more of a technique than a recipe), adapted slightly from the L.A. Times:

Russ Parson’s Dry-Brined Turkey (aka The Judy Bird)

Serves 11-15. Prep time 72 hours. Cook time 3 hours 15 minutes.

Ingredients:

  • One 12-16 pound turkey (frozen is fine)
  • Kosher salt
  • Herbs and/or spices for flavoring the salt (optional)
  • Melted butter for basting (optional)

Instructions:

1. Wash the turkey inside and out, pat it dry and weigh it. Measure 1 tablespoon of salt – Diamond Crystal Kosher is a good one, into a bowl for every 5 pounds the turkey weighs (for a 15-pound turkey, you’d have 3 tablespoons).You can flavor the salt with herbs and spices like a pinch of smoked paprika and orange zest, by leaf and thyme, or rosemary and lemon zest. Grind the herbs with the salt in a mortar and pestle or a small food processor.

2. Sprinkle the inside of the turkey lightly with salt. Place the turkey on its back and salt the breasts, concentrating the salt in the center, where the meat is thickest. You’ll probably use a little more than a tablespoon. It should look liberally seasoned, but not over-salted.

3. Turn the turkey on one side and sprinkle the entire side with salt, concentrating on the thigh. You should use a little less than a tablespoon. Flip the turkey over and do the same with the opposite side.

4. Place the turkey in a 2 1/2 gallon sealable plastic bag, press out the air and seal tightly. Place the turkey breast-side up in the refrigerator. Chill for three days, turning it onto its breast for the last day. Rub the salt around once a day, if you remember. Liquid may collect in the bag — this is normal.

5. For the crispiest skin, the night before, remove the turkey from the bag. There should be no salt visible on the surface and the skin should be moist but not wet. Place the turkey breast-side up on a plate and refrigerate uncovered for at least 8 hours.

6. On the day it is to be cooked, remove the turkey from the refrigerator and leave it at room temperature at least 1 hour. Do not rinse – it’s not needed and will make the skin less crispy. Preheat the oven to 425 degrees.

7. Pat it dry one last time and baste with melted butter, if using. Place the turkey breast-side down on a roasting rack in a roasting pan; put it in the oven. After 30 minutes, remove the pan from the oven and carefully turn the turkey over so the breast is facing up (it’s easiest to do this by hand, using kitchen towels or oven mitts).

8. Reduce the oven temperature to 325 degrees, return the turkey to the oven and roast until a thermometer inserted in the deepest part of the thigh, but not touching the bone, reads 165 degrees, about 2 3/4 hours total roasting. Note that because a dry-brined turkey cooks more quickly than one that hasn’t been brined, it’s best to check the temperature early with this recipe – it may be done faster than you realize!

9. Remove the turkey from the oven, transfer it to a warm platter or carving board; tent loosely with foil. Let stand at least 30 minutes to let the juices redistribute through the meat. Carve and serve.
